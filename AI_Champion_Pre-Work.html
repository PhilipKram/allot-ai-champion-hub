AI Foundations — Core Concepts

45 min

Read through these concepts. You don't need to memorize them — the goal is familiarity so we can build on them during the workshop.

What is a Large Language Model (LLM)?

An LLM is a machine learning model trained on vast amounts of text data. It predicts the most likely next word (token) given a sequence of input words. Key points:

  • It generates text, not truth. Output is statistically probable, not guaranteed to be correct.
  • It has no memory between conversations. Each new conversation starts fresh unless context is provided.
  • It works with a "context window." There's a limit to how much text it can process at once (think of it as working memory).
  • It can follow instructions. Modern LLMs are fine-tuned to respond helpfully to prompts.

The V-C-A-F Verification Framework — Preview

During the workshop, you'll learn and practice the V-C-A-F framework — the core habit every champion teaches their team. Here's a preview:

StepQuestion to Ask
VerifyIs this output factually correct? (Cross-reference against known data)
ContextualizeDoes this fit Allot's specific situation? (AI doesn't know our business)
AuthorizeAm I willing to put my name on this? (You own every AI output you use)
Fairness CheckIs this treating all groups/scenarios fairly? (AI can reflect biases from training data — critical for people-related analysis)

For routine tasks, V-C-A may suffice. Always include the Fairness Check when the output involves people, decisions, or analysis that could impact groups differently.

Apply this during Exercise 4 below — when you spot mistakes, you're already doing V-C-A-F.

First Steps with AI

45 min

Try these exercises using an approved AI tool (Claude recommended). The goal is to get comfortable with the interaction, not to produce perfect results.

Exercise 1: Basic Conversation 10 min

Ask the AI to:

  1. Explain what your team does in simple terms (as if to a new employee)
  2. Follow up by asking it to make the explanation more technical
  3. Then ask it to create 3 bullet points suitable for a slide deck
Notice: How the output changes based on your instructions. You're already prompting.

Exercise 2: Summarization 10 min

Find a recent document you've worked with (a spec, report, email thread, or meeting notes). Paste it into the AI and ask:

  • "Summarize this in 5 bullet points"
  • "What are the 3 most important action items?"
  • "Rewrite the summary for a non-technical audience"
Notice: How context and specificity affect the quality.

Exercise 3: The Power-User Prompt Template 15 min

Think about a repetitive task in your daily work. Turn it into a prompt using Role Assignment and Few-Shot Examples — two of the most effective advanced techniques:

ROLE: You are a [SPECIFIC ROLE, e.g., "Senior QA Engineer at Allot,
a network security company"]. Your expertise is in [DOMAINS].

TASK: I need you to [TASK].

CONTEXT:
- I work on [TEAM/DOMAIN] at Allot
- The input is [DESCRIBE INPUT]
- The audience is [WHO WILL READ THIS]

EXAMPLES (follow this style):
- Example 1 Input → Example 1 Output
- Example 2 Input → Example 2 Output

REQUIREMENTS:
- [REQUIREMENT 1]
- [REQUIREMENT 2]

FORMAT: [Specify: Markdown table, bullet points, numbered list, etc.]

Here is the input:
[PASTE INPUT]

Try it a few times. Notice how the Role and Examples improve output quality compared to a plain request.

Bring this prompt to the workshop — we'll refine it together.

Exercise 4: Spot the Mistakes — Apply V-C-A-F 10 min

Ask the AI a factual question about something you know well (your team's technology stack, a protocol you work with, or a tool you use daily). Then apply the V-C-A framework from Part 2:

  • Verify: Did it get the facts right? Where did it hallucinate?
  • Contextualize: Does the answer account for Allot's specific setup, or is it generic?
  • Authorize: Would you send this output to your manager as-is? What would you change?
  • Bonus — Red Team thinking: Could you trick the AI into giving a confidently wrong answer on this topic? Try rephrasing your question to mislead it. (On Day 1, you'll do a full "Red Team Your Prompts" exercise.)
This is the most important skill: knowing when to trust AI output and when to verify.

Exercise 5: Your First Prompt Chain 10 min

On Day 1, you'll build multi-step AI workflows. Try a simple one now:

  1. Step 1: Paste a short email or message into the AI and ask: "Extract all action items from this text as a bullet list"
  2. Step 2: Take the AI's output and ask: "For each action item, assign a priority (P1-Critical, P2-High, P3-Medium, P4-Low) and suggest a deadline"
  3. Step 3: Ask: "Format this as a table with columns: Action Item, Owner, Priority, Deadline"
Notice: Each step feeds the next. This is "prompt chaining" — a core technique for Day 1.
